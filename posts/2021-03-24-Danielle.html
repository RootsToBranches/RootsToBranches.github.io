Oshima-gun, Yamaguchi-ken Japan was once the home of my great grandparents Heijiro Hiraoka and Kise Nunota. The island is the 3rd largest in the Seto Inland Sea located in the eastern part of the Yamaguchi Prefecture in the Chugoku region. Nicknamed “the Island of Emigration” as early as 1885, due to its sudden over population and poor living conditions, many people from Oshima-gun left to settle in other parts of the world. Some moved to the Hawaiian islands to work on sugarcane plantations, and others like my great grandfather, immigrated to Canada to become fishermen.

Heijiro is thought to have moved to Canada as early as 1904 and became a naturalized Canadian in 1914. Kise immagrated to Canada around the same time and married Heijiro shortly after arriving. It is unclear whether or not Kise was a “picture bride”, a term that refers to Japanese women who were selected by a matchmaker to marry labourers who had emigrated to other parts of the world. Together they had four children, Chiyoko, my grandfather Masao, Natsuko and Kiyoshi. Kise split her time between Japan and Canada. My grandfather, studied in Japan as a teenager before returning to Canada to work with his father as a fisherman in Bamfield BC. The Hiraoka family home which was handcrafted by Heijiro and his sons still stands today.

My great grandfather, Kichitaro Yodogawa was born in Fukuoka-ken, Japan in 1877 and came to Canada around 1907. Legend has it he decided to come to Canada because his father had recently remarried and he wasn’t a huge fan of his new stepmother. In 1913 Kichitaro married my great grandmother Ichino who was only 17 years old at the time. Despite being so young Ichino had already been married once before and she was eager to move to Canada to start her new life in a new marriage. They settled in Steveston BC and lived in a 3 bedroom cannery house owned by the Imperial Cannery. They had seven children, Mitsuru, Fumiye, my grandmother Sumiko, Katsuko, Teruko, Setsuko and Tomiye.

My grandparents were married in 1942, the spring after the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbour during WWII. At this time Japanese Canadians living within 100 miles of the Pacific coast were being forcibly removed from their homes and placed in internment camps in BC’s interior. Roughly 21,000 people of Japanese decent were detained and dispossessed under the War Measures Act in order to “protect” the non Japanese Canadian public from the potential threat of Japanese espionage. Since my grandma was newly married she was separated from her siblings and parents and grouped together with her in-laws throughout the course of the internment. Men were relocated to “road camps” where they worked on a number of highway projects in both northern and southern BC. My grandfather’s family home and fishing boat were sold off at auction for a fraction their worth. The property and possessions of Japanese Canadians interned during the war were never returned.

During the war my grandmother gave birth to my uncles Dick and Frank, who are only a year apart in age. Even though life in the internment camps was difficult it is evident from family photos that my grandparents and young uncles did what they could to enjoy time with family and life’s simple pleasures. After the war ended my grandparents and their families moved to Manitoba to work on a sugarbeet farm in order to earn back some of the money they had lost as a result of the internment. They later settled in Winnipeg Manitoba where my mother was born on March 22, 1957..

My parents met while working together at a computer company called Digital Equipment Corporation. From a young age my mother educated my brother and I about the struggles my grandparents faced as Japanese Canadians living in Canada during WWII. She instilled in us the idea that our mixed race identity was multifaceted and ensured that we understood we didn’t need to choose whether we were Japanese, French or Canadian, we could proudly be all 3.
